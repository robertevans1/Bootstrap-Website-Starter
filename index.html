Bikepacking Georgia 2018

The nation of Georgia lies on the frontier of both geographical and cultural divides; straddled by the Black and Caspian seas, East meets West, Europe meets Asia, and Russia and the Middle East both loom large at the borders. This geopolitical conjuncture has lead to Georgia seeing more than its fair share of conflict over the centuries, most recently with dispute over the region of South Ossetia sparking a month of fighting in 2008.

The north of the country is dominated by a large part of Europe’s highest mountain range; the Caucasus Mountains. For me and two friends, the allure of these mountains and promise of adventure were great enough that we found ourselves and our bikes flying in to Georgia’s capital Tbilisi, in July of 2018. Our plan was to bikepack a route over a couple of high mountain passes, following a combination of dirt roads and footpaths. We aimed to stick closely to the a section of a longer cross caucasus bikepacking route. We would travel unassisted, carrying a tent and some freeze dried food rations, aiming to stock up water and extra provisions whenever possible.

What followed were 7 days of spectacular riding, some gruelling uphill bike pushes, a couple of run-ins with the notorious Tushetian sheep dogs, some sketchy river fordings and certainly the adventure we were looking for. Below are some notes and photos from the ride.

Day 1: Chargali to Roshka

We took a 4x4 for a couple of hours north from Tbilisi, we were dropped at the side a of a dirt road near the village of Chargali. A couple of mechanical issues assembling the bikes had us nervous we weren’t even going to be able to start. The 35km ride was largely an uphill grind. With the last section following up some hot steep switchback turns to the village of Roshka.

Day 2: Roshka to Shatili

We enjoyed the descent from Roshka to the main road. After that it was a steady climb toward Shatili for 15km.

We stopped at Biso which had dozens of beehives by the road, buying a tub of honey from the ramshackle farmhouse. Soon after we started climbing switchback turns for an hour or two, gaining 600m elevation. The honey helped us reach the top!

Glorious descent along the road on the other side down to river. Then very pleasant descent following the river to Shatili.

We explored the interesting old fortress in Shatili. Then we paused at green house hotel to try some Khinkali, georgian dumplings stuffed with pork, freshly made by the friendly grandmother who runs the kitchen with the help of the extended family. We were invited to join the family to watch last half of England's world cup game.

Day 3: Shatili to the Asunta Pass

The ride started easily enough, passing Mutka, a hamlet with the ruins of an old fort high above. Got papers at military checkpoint. In places our path here was less than a kilometre from the Russian border.

Finally we reached a spectacular camping area at 2800m.

Day 4: Asunta Pass to Girevi

Woke early and got straight back into pushing the bikes. The final 600m of ascent was tough, very steep, scree underfoot, and thin air approaching the col at 3500m.

Descent began with steep scree fields, only half ridable.

Some great single track sections on the descent with consistly stunning views, with a few uphill pushes, thorn bushes and river fordings thrown in.

Hoping to press on it seemed the only way to continue was via the river crossing. Thew water was cold, nearly waist deep and fast moving. I fell in with my bike and bags on the first crossing, Rob and James threw bags over and then made it across. One of James' bags landed in a fresh pile of horse poo.

Our joy at making it across was short lived, we found out a second crossing was required lower down but river looked even stronger, ended up fording back across the original. Everyone fell in. Cold and wet we decided to turn in for the day and headed to the nearest guesthouse.

Day 5: Girevi to Omalo

Avoided fording the river again leaving Girevi, instead taking a walking path. Cruisey down hill roads took us quickly to Dartlo following the river. From Dartlo we climbed some way before descending to Omalo. The descent along the dirt road was a highlight, fast and flowey with a few bumps and rollers. We made it to Omalo in time for lunch, for which we had an excellent Georgian Fondue.

In the afternoon we relaxed and visited the old stone towers overlooking the town.

Day 6: Omalo area

We stayed in Omalo for the day. Enjoying riding without the bags for a change. We cruised down from High Omalo to Diklo via a footpath. Hot and sweaty, around 30 degrees. But the dusty mud roads were fun riding.

Day 7: Omalo to Telavi

Woke at 430am to ride before the heat. Made good progress and had breakfast just before the start of the albano pass. Climbed 1200m on switchbacks.

After the climb we were rewarded with an incredible and seemingly never ending descent. General consensus rated it as the best road we'd ever ridden. The heat increased to 40C as we reached the valley. Riding the last 45km to Telavi in that heat was obscenely sweaty!

Winemaking is thought to have originated in Georgia in around 6000bc, and as Telavi is the heart of Georgian wine country we found time the next day to sample some of the local vintages, it seemed to perfect way to finish an fantastic trip!
